General Visa Questions

An eVisa (electronic visa) is a travel authorization issued digitally. You apply online, pay the fee, and receive approval by email. No embassy visit is needed. Countries like Turkey, India, Vietnam, Cambodia, and Australia offer eVisas.

A traditional visa requires visiting an embassy or consulate, submitting paper documents, and sometimes attending an interview. An eVisa is applied for entirely online, processed faster (often within 24-72 hours), and delivered electronically to your email.

An ETA is a pre-travel screening for citizens of visa-exempt countries. It is not a visa but a travel authorization linked to your passport. Countries like Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and the UK use ETA systems. The EU's ETIAS system launches in 2026.

Visa-free travel means you can enter a country without applying for a visa in advance. You simply present your passport at immigration. The allowed stay is usually 30-90 days. Strong passports (Japan, Singapore, EU) offer visa-free access to 190+ countries.

A visa-on-arrival is issued at the port of entry (airport or border crossing) without prior application. You present your passport, fill out a form, pay the fee, and receive the visa stamp. Countries like Indonesia, Nepal, and Jordan offer VOA for many nationalities.

Visa requirements depend on your nationality and destination. Use our visa search tool on the homepage or check the specific country page. Generally, citizens of strong passport countries (EU, US, UK, Japan) enjoy visa-free access to 150+ countries.

As of 2026, Japan, Singapore, and several EU passports consistently rank strongest, offering visa-free or visa-on-arrival access to 190+ countries. The Henley Passport Index and Arton Capital index rank passports quarterly based on travel freedom.

Schengen & European Visas

A Schengen visa allows travel to 27 European countries with a single visa. It is valid for short stays of up to 90 days in any 180-day period. You apply at the embassy of the country you will visit most (or enter first). See our France, Germany, Spain, and Italy visa pages.

As of 2026, the Schengen Area includes 27 countries: Austria, Belgium, Croatia, Czech Republic, Denmark, Estonia, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Iceland, Italy, Latvia, Liechtenstein, Lithuania, Luxembourg, Malta, Netherlands, Norway, Poland, Portugal, Romania, Slovakia, Slovenia, Spain, Sweden, and Switzerland.

Yes. A Schengen visa allows free movement across all 27 Schengen countries during your authorized stay (up to 90 days in 180 days). You must apply at the embassy of your main destination or first entry country.

The 90/180 rule means you can stay in the Schengen Area for a maximum of 90 days within any 180-day period. This is calculated on a rolling basis. Once you've used 90 days, you must leave and wait until enough days have "expired" before re-entering.

ETIAS (European Travel Information and Authorisation System) is a new pre-travel authorization for visa-exempt travelers visiting the Schengen Area. It launches in 2026. Citizens of 60+ countries (US, UK, Canada, Australia, Japan) will need ETIAS approval (EUR 7, valid 3 years) before traveling to Europe.

Yes, but your Schengen visa should be issued by the country of your main destination (longest stay). If you enter through a different country, immigration may question your itinerary. If all stays are equal, apply to the country of first entry.

Documents & Application Process

Common requirements: valid passport (6+ months validity), passport photos, completed application form, travel itinerary, hotel reservation, proof of financial means (bank statements), travel insurance, and invitation letter (if applicable). See our documents checklist.

Most countries require 35x45mm photos with a white background. The US requires 51x51mm (2x2 inches). Photos must be recent (within 6 months), show a neutral expression, and meet specific lighting requirements. See our visa photo requirements guide.

Yes, for Schengen visas you must have travel insurance with minimum EUR 30,000 coverage. Many other countries also require or strongly recommend it. See our travel insurance guide.

Most countries require your passport to be valid for at least 6 months beyond your planned stay. Schengen countries require 3 months beyond your departure date plus two blank pages. Always check the specific country's requirements.

For Schengen visas, show EUR 50-100 per day of stay. For the US B1/B2, demonstrate sufficient funds (no fixed amount). For the UK, show 3-6 months of consistent income. Generally, show a bank balance covering your trip costs plus 30-50% buffer.

Most visa applications require proof of accommodation for your entire stay. This can be a hotel reservation (not necessarily pre-paid), Airbnb booking, or a letter from your host. For Schengen visas, all nights must be covered.

Most countries require proof of onward or return travel for visa applications and at immigration. This can be a confirmed flight booking, bus ticket, or ferry ticket showing you plan to leave before your visa expires.

An invitation letter is a document from a person or organization in the destination country inviting you to visit. It includes the host's details, your relationship, purpose and dates of visit, accommodation arrangements, and financial responsibility. Required for many Schengen and business visa applications.

Biometrics include fingerprint scans and a digital photograph collected during your visa application. Schengen countries, the UK, US, Canada, and many others require biometrics. They are stored for identity verification and are usually valid for 5 years.

Fees & Processing Times

Visa fees vary widely. Examples: Schengen visa EUR 80, US B1/B2 visa USD 185, India eVisa USD 25-80, Turkey eVisa USD 50, Australia ETA AUD 20, Cambodia eVisa USD 36. Children and some nationalities may qualify for reduced fees. See our processing times page.

eVisas typically take 24-72 hours. Schengen visas take 15-45 calendar days. US B1/B2 visas can take weeks to months depending on the embassy. Always apply well in advance of your travel date.

For eVisas: 1-2 weeks before travel. For Schengen visas: 3-6 months in advance (earliest 6 months, latest 15 days before departure). For US visas: 3-6 months or more due to long wait times. Peak travel seasons may require earlier applications.

In most cases, visa fees are non-refundable regardless of the outcome. The fee covers the cost of processing your application, not a guarantee of approval.

Most embassies and VFS Global offices provide online tracking. You'll receive a reference number when you submit. Enter this number on the embassy or VFS website. Some countries also send email/SMS updates at each processing stage.

VFS Global is a private company that processes visa applications on behalf of governments. They operate visa application centers (VACs) worldwide. You submit documents at a VFS center instead of the embassy directly. VFS charges a service fee on top of the visa fee.

Extensions, Overstay & Refusal

It depends on the country and visa type. Some countries allow extensions (e.g., Indonesia VOA can be extended 30 days, Thailand tourist visa can be extended 30 days). Schengen visas can only be extended in exceptional circumstances. See our visa rejection reasons page for related info.

You will receive a written notification with the reason. Common reasons include insufficient financial proof, incomplete documents, or suspected immigration intent. You can usually appeal within 1-3 months or reapply with improved documentation.

Overstaying means remaining beyond your authorized stay. Consequences include fines (e.g., Thailand charges 500 THB/day), deportation, detention, entry bans (1-10 years), and difficulty obtaining future visas. Always respect your visa validity dates.

Contact your country's nearest embassy immediately for an emergency travel document. You will need to apply for a new visa, as visas in lost passports are considered void. File a police report and keep a copy for the new application.

Special Visa Types

A transit visa allows you to pass through a country on your way to your final destination. It is usually valid for 24-72 hours. Some countries require transit visas even if you don't leave the airport.

No. Tourist visas generally do not permit employment. Working on a tourist visa is illegal in most countries and can result in deportation, fines, and future visa denials. You need a separate work visa or work permit.

A digital nomad visa allows remote workers to live in a country while working for employers or clients abroad. Popular countries include Portugal, Spain, Thailand, Colombia, and Indonesia. See our digital nomad visa guide.

A student visa allows you to study at an educational institution in another country. Requirements include an acceptance letter, proof of tuition payment or scholarship, financial means for living expenses, health insurance, and sometimes language proficiency certificates.

A Working Holiday Visa (WHV) allows young people (usually 18-30 or 18-35) to travel and work in another country for 1-2 years. Popular programs include Australia, New Zealand, Canada, and Japan.

A Golden Visa is a residence permit granted to investors who make a significant financial investment (usually real estate or business). Popular programs exist in Portugal, Spain, Greece, and the UAE. Investments range from EUR 250,000 to EUR 500,000+.

A Type D visa (national visa) allows stays longer than 90 days in a specific country. Unlike the Schengen short-stay visa (Type C), a Type D is issued by individual countries for purposes like study, work, family reunification, or retirement.

A multiple-entry visa allows you to enter and exit a country multiple times during the visa's validity. This is useful for business travelers or people visiting neighboring countries. Single-entry visas become invalid after you leave the country once.

Embassy & Application Process

Steps: 1) Check requirements on the embassy website. 2) Gather documents. 3) Book an appointment. 4) Attend and submit documents. 5) Pay the fee. 6) Wait for processing. 7) Collect your passport with the visa. See our how to apply guide.

Some countries (notably the US, UK, and Canada) require a personal interview at the embassy. The consular officer will ask about your travel plans, ties to your home country, financial situation, and purpose of visit. Be honest and bring supporting documents.

A visa sticker is a physical label affixed to a passport page (Schengen, US, UK). A visa stamp is an entry/exit mark stamped by immigration. An e-visa is a digital authorization linked to your passport number — no physical sticker needed.
